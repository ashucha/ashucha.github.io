About

Philosophy

My experiences as a servant leader and as a young professional have guided my pursuits and attitude toward how I conduct and guide myself and others. At every opportunity I get, I try to help someone out, be it on something as simple as doing homework or as complex as fixing their phone. In addition to this, I make sure to encourage them to do the same for others. By spreading the spirit of helping others, I hope to develop a community which proactively addresses and solves its problems through a collective effort as a united community with common goals can tackle any issue, no matter the magnitude.

Future Goals

Throughout my high school career, I have consistently sought opportunities to further my leadership skills and experience. In addition to being a Student Leader as part of Student Leadership Johns Creek and a Student Ambassador for the Georgia Secretary of State's Office, I am the Co-Founder and Captain of Northview High School's Science Bowl Team and Vice President of the Northview Programming Club. Each of these experiences has carried with it a different set of expectations ranging from managing a team to teaching a large group to leading volunteering efforts. From these opportunities, I have gained invaluable lessons that have not only helped develop me as a leader but also as a young professional, and I believe that they will help me in my future pursuits to become an AI engineer with a degree in AI from a prestigious tech university.

Personal Mission & Vision

From my leadership experiences, I have learned some key values of a leader that I carry with me. Primarily, I strive to be a leader who is not always be the smartest one in the room. I will listen to those around me who are more knowledgeable and take the appropriate course of action based on those inputs. In a world where one cannot be the best at everything, this is an essential value of a leader.

Reflection

On March 14th, 2019, I received the email I had been eagerly waiting for for weeks. I was excited to be accepted into Student Leadership Johns Creek’s Class of 2021. Little did I know how much this two-year program would change me both as a community leader and as a professional.

Soon, I found myself thrown into leading a group project at The Drake House to teach advanced computer skills and concepts to the children there, kids who were worrying about their next meal and separation from family. Ironically, I learned more from the children, about the power of positivity, determination, and extending a helping hand, than I taught them about computers and digital technology. From this experience, I recognized the impact that I could have and desired to use my skills to make a difference in my community.

Last summer, while scrolling through my neighborhood’s Nextdoor, I learned that many parents in my community were struggling to engage their children in fun and educational activities during the pandemic. My previous experience at The Drake House pushed me to get together with a few friends to teach coding to these kids. Our bootcamp, called Code Up!, was a success, drawing hundreds of children from across the country. This was when I truly realized the potential of SLJC to help me grow as a leader. When I first heard that we were doing a portfolio project, I thought I was set. With a LinkedIn profile and resume set up, I felt that this would be a walk in the park for me. However, as I learned more and more about the various aspects of and considerations for a professional identity, I realized how much of a challenge this project would be.

First off, I was unsure about which specific path within the vast field of computer science I would pursue. Split between web development and AI, I started researching the career paths as well as my interests through the StrengthsQuest survey. When I got my results, I realized that AI would be the best path for me.

Next, I needed to figure out what to use for my portfolio site. Would I use Weebly as a quick and dirty but extremely primitive solution, or would I code it, toiling through the long and arduous task of building and deploying but learning about web deployment along the way? I chose the road less traveled by and decided to code it, a decision I will never regret.

Although it required a great deal more work and effort to achieve the same results as using Weebly, coding my site made the otherwise long task of making an online portfolio much more fun and engaging as I was able to customize various aspects of my site like transitions and mouse pointer animations. I also gained a lot of invaluable technical knowledge along the way. For example, I learned how to add a custom domain to my site for free, get a free SSL certificate to make connections to my site more secure, and host my site on Google to bolster my portfolio. In addition to technical knowledge, I learned much about maintaining an LinkedIn profile, like what to list where, how to describe work experiences, making an effective headline. I also learned how to make a business card, sell myself to recruiters by choosing the right words to describe me, and connect with other people to expand my network of contacts.

As I neared the completion of my project, I realized how much I had changed since I started working on it. From a simple, nearly empty LinkedIn page, I now have a full-fledged profile as well as a portfolio website. My choice of career has also narrowed down to an AI engineer and I have taken steps to brand myself as such. I am not only grateful for the opportunities that this portfolio project has afforded me, but also how SLJC has changed me as a person. From a high schooler uncertain about his future, I have changed into a student leader planning for his career, something that I have realized sets me apart from my peers, and a transformation that would only have been possible as part of SLJC’s class of 2021.
